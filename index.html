ABOUT US


HARRISON & JUSTICE CHILDREN'S HOME (HJCH) is a non-governmental organization working as a child refuge and adoption center founded in 2004 in Kitale, Kenya. HJCH is mandated by the local social services and children's department supported by an existing court order to provide shelter, care and rehabilitation programs to destitute, homeless children in Kitale.
HJCH is periodically vetted by the social services and children's department to ensure adherence to safety standards for orphanages.

As part of its rehabilitation program, the home provides food, shelter, clothing, medical support, guiding and counseling services, and educational support up to high school level for all children.
While the home depends on donors and well-wishers to support its orphanage programs, it is looking to grow sustainably by undertaking farming and business activities to generate income.



OUR HISTORY


HARRISON & JUSTICE CHILDREN'S HOME started as a simple idea to provide foster care to three teenage boys who had lost their parents in 2000 in Kital town. In 2001, Laban Wayoga, the founder of HJCH, took up the three boys aged 8 to 12, and though single and unemployed at the time, made every effort to provide food and shelter to them.
He found some support from a missionary couple from Texas, USA, who were then running Calvary Baptist Church, and they partnered to educate the boys. HJCH was officially set up as an orphanage in 2004 after seeing a successful outcome with the three boys, who have gone to become successful at their young careers, with one of the boys having been privileged to attend and graduate from Duke University in the USA owing to his excellent academic performance.
He is currently giving back to the home in many ways.
Laban set up HJCH as a dedication to his two late brothers (Harrison a former US resident in Atlanta and Justice a former legal advocate in Kitale) who had dedicated themselves to social and charity work in their communities.
Laban saw an opportunity to continue their legacy by providing a center for hope and inspiration for orphaned kids, many who had lost their parents to HIV/AIDS, poverty and domestic violence. Laban, currently a certified theologian, and his wife Norah have dedicated the rest of their lives to supporting destitute children realize their potential and future.
